# Champion Kit

A structured approach for individual engineers advocating Claude Code within their organization. The champion role is defined by three reinforcing behaviors: sharing discoveries publicly, answering questions with concrete prompts, and growing the circle through lightweight recurring habits. The role is designed to be a multiplier on existing work rather than an additional support responsibility. ^[raw/document/claude code/claude-code-037-champion-kit-2026-04-29.md]

## Key Points

- Three champion behaviors: (1) share what you discover by posting prompts and wins where the team already reads, (2) be the person people ask by answering with the actual prompt rather than an explanation, (3) grow the circle through recurring habits so momentum continues without a single point of failure ^[raw/document/claude code/claude-code-037-champion-kit-2026-04-29.md]
- Weekly time investment: ~15 minutes posting wins, ~20 minutes answering questions in a shared channel, ~5 minutes for a weekly show-and-tell thread, and 0–30 minutes for optional pairing sessions ^[raw/document/claude code/claude-code-037-champion-kit-2026-04-29.md]
- The most useful posts describe reusable techniques (e.g., @-mentioning a directory to find missing tests) rather than completed outcomes; techniques compound as they spread ^[raw/document/claude code/claude-code-037-champion-kit-2026-04-29.md]
- Post where the team already reads (engineering channels, PR descriptions, standups) rather than creating a new destination ^[raw/document/claude code/claude-code-037-champion-kit-2026-04-29.md]
- When someone asks how you did something, share the prompt you actually used so they can run it against their own problem immediately ^[raw/document/claude code/claude-code-037-champion-kit-2026-04-29.md]
- The colleague who asks the most questions is usually ready to become the next champion ^[raw/document/claude code/claude-code-037-champion-kit-2026-04-29.md]

## Details

The champion role is explicitly framed as a multiplier rather than a help desk. The goal is for each shared example to shorten the learning curve for others, and for each public answer to turn individual experience into team-wide knowledge. Posts should be short enough to absorb at a glance — a screenshot plus one or two lines of context — because long write-ups tend to be saved for later and forgotten, while short posts with screenshots tend to be copied and tried. ^[raw/document/claude code/claude-code-037-champion-kit-2026-04-29.md]

The thirty-day playbook structures adoption in four phases. Week 1 seeds the channel with two or three personal examples. Week 2 starts a weekly show-and-tell thread and shares a custom skill or CLAUDE.md snippet. Week 3 consolidates common questions into a pinned FAQ and offers short pairing sessions. Week 4 identifies a second champion and hands off. The signal that the role has succeeded is when questions in the channel are answered by people other than the original champion. ^[raw/document/claude code/claude-code-037-champion-kit-2026-04-29.md]

When responding to common concerns, the recommended approach is to acknowledge the concern, offer a brief reframe, and propose one concrete demonstration on the person's own code rather than arguing the general case. For security and data-handling questions, champions should refer to the organization's administrator and not improvise answers. ^[raw/document/claude code/claude-code-037-champion-kit-2026-04-29.md]
